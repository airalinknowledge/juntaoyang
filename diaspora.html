Does the Expansion of the Meaning of "Diaspora" Soften Historical Violence?
November 11, 2025
In Jewish theology, "diaspora" is understood as a people punished with exile and dispersion for having forsaken the Law. In the cultural studies of the 1980s, "diaspora" was reinterpreted within the context of imperialism and colonial history as referring to dispersed peoples, a concept bound up with enslavement and displacement. Loss of homeland, forced migration, collective trauma, and state violence are the keywords of this classical interpretation.
However, newer research and popular discourse have added numerous new typologies to this more classical definition, placing emphasis on conditions of dispersion based on labor, trade, and education. We are witnessing an increasing number of well-educated sojourners (for instance, the younger generation of Chinese artists in Euro-American art schools) beginning to identify as "diasporic," sometimes aestheticizing this condition.
Yet, unlike the "diaspora" defined by scholars like Rogers Brubaker¹—which is founded on forced and traumatic dispersion—these new "diasporic" subjects have not experienced genuine violence or carried a specific collective trauma. More often, these subjects possess privileged conditions, flexible channels of mobility, and the perpetual possibility of returning to their ancestral homeland. I am not attempting to deny their cultural anxiety or emotional isolation; rather, I wish to point out that this reversible dispersion has become an attractive—and at times, superior—option.
In this self-identification of the diasporic subject, the components that were originally more injurious and fraught with historical violence seem to have vanished. For this demographic, the porosity of borders in the globalized era has shifted from abrasive to frictionless; consequently, they have never experienced the lacerations and bruising universally endured by earlier diasporic groups.
I would even argue that this has become a form of "chic." People enjoy inhabiting the "nomadic subject"—a concept I find distinctly romanticized in the work of Rosi Braidotti.² For Braidotti, the free-flowing elite class (and capital itself) and expelled refugees are both subsumed within the nomadic subject; such a generalization, lacking in ethical dimensions, is staggering. Accompanying the proliferation of this terminology, the dimensions of power asymmetry and trauma within the diasporic condition have been softened.





1. Rogers Brubaker, "The 'diaspora' diaspora," Ethnic and Racial Studies 28, no. 1 (2005): 1-19.
2. Rosi Braidotti, Nomadic Subjects: Embodiment and Sexual Difference in Contemporary Feminist Theory (Columbia University Press, 1994).
