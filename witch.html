Salem: Camp, Capital, and the Good Witch
February 17, 2026
Salem is remote, quiet, and anemic. This northern, decaying yet recalcitrant ice and snow has a knack for muting the city. Amidst the tight, seamless masonry of New England red brick, those Gen Z-esque signboards display a perverse taste, wearing the city’s violent history like a badge of honor—garish, covetous, mimicking hand-drawn styles with digital fonts. Within this atmosphere of Puritanical architectural asceticism, these tastes of witchcraft and pop-culture shops beckon, with an almost coquettish posture, to the dismissive, thrill-seeking Goth tourists. Being drawn here by the Bewitched slogan—enchanted by Sylvia and others—I suppose I am one of them.
Seagulls circle overhead; slush and mud churn beneath tire tracks. Bypassing the mall emblazoned with "Witch City Mall," one hits Essex St., with the Chestnut St. District to the west and Derby Wharf to the east. The sea, shimmering with an oil-slick sheen, vanishes into the ice near the shore; it is almost impossible to imagine its state when this was the wealthiest city in North America. Situated at a subtle distance from the Peabody Essex Museum—that top-tier repository of maritime trade wealth—the Salem Witch Museum stands as the city's largest tourist trap.
I suspect this dark-room light show, accompanied by tacky, cheap sound effects and posturing narration, has not been updated since it was designed in 1972, fueled by the currents of pop culture. It is clearly not the premier chaser of clichéd stories with mass appeal, yet neither has it suffered from the shrinking mass market caused by the continuous disenchantment of progressive movements. The black nanny bearing exotic knowledge, the hysterical girls and the blind masses, the rational, brave white farmer sacrificing himself tragically to defend his wife, and religious atonement—these figures have existed in every popular Salem narrative since Arthur Miller. They have been absorbed, unscathed, into a context defended (and thus sanitized) by a generation of feminist and anti-racist scholars.
The exhibition arranged after the show, concerning the evolution of the witch image and the political mobilization of its discourse, is clearly not devoid of educational value. Yet, the anxieties of conservatives quietly vanish into the ticket revenue collected from this audience, who watch with half-mocking detachment. Perhaps the truth is this: the political potential of Camp has been overestimated. Or perhaps another truth: the ravenous appetite beneath the benign face of cultural consumption has been underestimated. Right-wing atrocities can be digested; left-wing serious debates can be digested; even the postmodern playfulness toward both can be digested. We have (once again) stepped into the post-post; there is no Benjaminian dyad here—the politicization of Camp vs. the campification of politics—there is only a chain equation: Culture = Politics = Capital, where the final equals sign turns all preceding terms into placeholders.
This text itself does not fall outside of it. In fact, queer theory today has expanded into a universal metaphor for a critique of totality, albeit as a helpless move against that leviathan. Wang Min'an wrote that "queer" is a quality unrecognized and untamed by political norms; thus, "all that is mortal is queer." How do we treat this radical gesture, rich in aesthetic yet already diluted and decaying? Does there still exist a fright (a surprise?) unanticipated by us (and thus by capital)? When I witnessed that Asian girl’s excessive fright caused by the crudeness of a wax figure—a reaction exceeding the script of solemn awe or campy amusement—did it hint at some residue that totality cannot subsume, enough to redeem our critical enterprise?
While loitering in hunger, I passed a shop named The Good Witch. It lacked the stereotypical bruise-purple palette, filled instead with cute pink toys. This pink strongly evoked the visual style of the character recently played by Ariana Grande. But the fissure here is: what is, and why is it, a "Good Witch"? When Glinda appeared as a Good Witch in 1900, Baum actually rejected the harmless "fairy," choosing instead to re-acknowledge and appropriate the potential danger of the word "witch." It must be admitted that the re-appropriation of the word queer is historically more complex than what Baum did. However, the problem lies in the fact that the antagonism of queer still relies on its binary posture standing directly opposite straight. This straight-queer opposition is, ultimately, appropriated by capital operating dialectically. In other words, capital unifies the redneck emotional economy and pink capitalism within the same synthesis.
The setup of Oz is different. There are only four witches, and no opposing category of "fairies." "Witch" itself becomes the ontological substrate, which of course implies that we are all witches—another beautifully gestured radical slogan, but it is more than that. I find it seems to signify an anti-dialectical operation of immanence: not opposing another category from the outside, but discovering a potential heterogeneous mode within a singular interiority. These Spinozan operations of immanence do, to some extent, evade the potential risk of the dialectical machine: devouring the opposite, synthesizing it, and spitting out a commodity. Inventing or claiming a category that fundamentally does not rely on opposition seems to indeed discover a short circuit in this dialectical machine. Lines of flight on the same plane are ceaseless; the implosion of this category also requires continuous self-operation, constantly emerging with new intensities within the fragile territory of the Good Witch, to prevent it from evolving into another set of zombie dyads: good witch vs. bad witch. It is an escape awakened by different gradients of intensity within the same thing, not by a negation.
But is this escape too far-fetched? Its garish pink inherits not just Deleuze's legacy, but the texture of the capitalist commodity economy deeply etched into its genes. Nevertheless, at least in this moment, on the edge of Salem, let us, for now, be Good Witches.
