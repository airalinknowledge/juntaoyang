Media Studies as a Contemporary Theoretical Agenda
February 17, 2026
The ontological question of media is the truly serious matter of contemporary media studies. It is often criticized for interpreting everything as a medium or mediation—as process, movement, event. In fact, such interpretation often results in an awkward stance regarding political positioning: when "everything is media," what significance does this category retain? Can it still identify the imbalances and asymmetries within social dynamics? I would not argue that this is a problem of media studies per se, but rather insist that it is a characteristic—or, if you will, a weakness—of what I term "almost-high-theory" itself. In Althusser and his acolytes, everything is articulated within the framework of ideology, to the extent that everything becomes an ideology: speech is ideology, the subject is ideology, ritual-action is ideology. In strict Foucaultianism, everything is power (often manifesting as discourse). In the Birmingham School, every object or symptom awaiting analysis is viewed by the collective intellectual creators as a "culture."
Today's media studies is much the same. If you recall what I once attempted to argue—that today's media studies is actually another confluence of British Cultural Studies, German Theory, and French Critical Theory within the United States—then it is not surprising why it stands as the quasi-heir to this position of "almost-high-theory." So, can the concept of mediation uphold the ethical baseline of justice? One need only look at the book manuscript of the young NYU scholar Whit Pow to know: Mediation there retraces the moment Kittler organized the translation of Foucault's collected works. I mean to say that mediation is both a technology of discretization, gridding, simulation, and isolation from the real (always as a non-representational representation)—think of the hallucination of the photograph discussed by Stiegler and constantly appearing in Roland Barthes: the closer you get to the screen, the less real what you see becomes, revealing instead increasingly discrete and obvious pixels—and, consequently, a technology of power for framing, abstraction, statistics, and arrangement. Pow's compelling case involves the medical imaging imposed on transgender individuals: a method of mediation that disguises its own scientific reproducibility as non-representational, while simultaneously acting as a mechanism of power that mediates people into categories, data, and other forms of discreteness.
Thus, you see, mediation is always closely related to power.
