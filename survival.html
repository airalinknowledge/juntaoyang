The Grammar of Survival, or after floods, we still love.
October 24, 2025
What is the grammar of survival? It is fawning, complicity, and a desperate struggle to transfer lethal pressure onto other, weaker beings in exchange for one's own continued existence. It is an accumulation and cycle of harm upon harm. The ethics of survival face an interrogation: Which unscrupulous means of survival are acceptable? And which are not? Which, due to their excessive complexity, fall into specific contexts beyond binary judgment, awaiting thick description and an attempt at understanding? The language of survival is always related to those who were never permitted to survive. Therefore, to survive means to eternally bear the weight of all other lives that were broken, lost, and debased. This is a Benjaminian dangerous moment, a flashning to be seized: we are invested with a tiny messianic power, which demands that we redeem the predecessors who lay a claim on us. How much of what you owe must be remembered and testified to, in order to justify the fact of your own survival?
How do we take survival—that dark, mutually destructive, constantly mutating state of extremity under pressure—as the foundation for a potential alliance? If this shared experience, in all its complexity that refuses a happy ending, is our only starting point, then how do we find hope amidst a fragmented and cold capriciousness? Perhaps one method is about how to seriously seduce as many beings as possible.
To hook, to seduce or to link—this is about an ethics of connection, about desire, about love. I am fascinated by this idea of seduction. It is different from catering to another or waiting to be pursued; it is the hand that actively reaches out, which also means opening oneself to danger, signifying a certain vulnerability and a ceding of ground. This is an adventure, an act of ceding and transcending the self’s desire. In this moment, the individual remains lucid in their desire, yet at the critical point, cedes that desire to allow the Other to appear. The Other, in a Levinasian sense, ethically precedes me at every turn, yet ontologically withdraws from me. Thus, this is not a sacrifice: sacrifice is a human attempt to bribe the gods, but the gods do not accept bribes; they have their own uninterruptible rhythms, and disaster will still arrive.
The gods are a metaphor for when humanity attempts to grasp things that transcend the human. “Grasp” is too much of a phenomenological word; it seems too forceful, too mired in possession. Seduction, by contrast, is a probing, the moment just before two hands touch. Harman tends to believe that objects always withdraw. Grasping can only be a futile effort. It has the potential to become an extractivist paranoia, but also an ethical practice of building understanding. This ambiguity arises because, to a certain extent, the Other is something that can never be fully and completely grasped. It is not what we perceive in the phenomenal realm, but something that is always ontologically withdrawn, residing in obscurity. In its temporality, it is that which always comes before; we feel it in a sense of delay, but only as a touch, a tremor on the skin. Disaster is something transcendent.
But the effort to grasp can also be converted into a form of understanding and attunement. This word refers to a capacity for “coexisting with.” It means that even if heavy rains flood Tokyo, humanity still survives, cherry blossoms still bloom, animals still live, and people still love and pray. This is our attitude when facing the obscurity of the Other or of nature. We cannot penetrate it, cannot control it; we can only remain open, negotiating with it in a state of vulnerable exposure. This effort demands that we continue to love after disaster, harm, and violence. The concept of seduction is important because we are trying to survive in a continuously damaged world, to maintain connection amidst a vast uncontrollability, in desire and in tenderness.
After Auschwitz, we must still write poetry, just as after the flood, we still love.
This is a story of care and trauma, and also a story of how to survive after catastrophe. Beneath the scars of heaven and earth, we attempt to cross over together. And that is why we must tell this story stubbornly, resolutely, over and over again.
